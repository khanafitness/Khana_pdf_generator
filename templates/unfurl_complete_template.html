Women's Anatomy & Pleasure Map
The female body contains one of the most complex pleasure systems in human anatomy.
The Clitoris — Far More Than You Think

The visible tip of the clitoris is just the beginning. The full clitoral structure extends 9–11cm inside the body, with internal legs (crura) and bulbs wrapping around the vaginal canal. It contains approximately 8,000 nerve endings — more than any other human structure — and exists solely for pleasure. Much of what we call "vaginal orgasm" is actually the internal clitoris being indirectly stimulated.

The clitoris has no reproductive function. It exists entirely for pleasure — which is, in itself, a remarkable biological fact.

The G-Spot

Located 5–7cm inside the vagina on the front wall. Connected to the internal clitoris, urethral sponge, and Skene's glands. Stimulation here can lead to intense orgasm and, for some, ejaculation.

The A-Spot

The Anterior Fornix Erogenous zone — located deeper than the G-spot, near the cervix. Less widely known but highly responsive in many women. Can produce intense lubrication rapidly.

The Cervix

For some women, deep cervical pressure produces a distinct, full-body orgasmic sensation. For others it can be uncomfortable. Always approach slowly and with communication.

The Glans Clitoris

The external, visible part. The most nerve-dense point on the body. For most women, direct stimulation here is the most reliable path to orgasm.

Female Ejaculation & The Orgasm Gap

Research confirms ejaculation occurs from the Skene's glands. The fluid is a combination of PSA, glucose, and small amounts of urine — it is not urine. Studies show only 30% of women orgasm every time, vs. 61% of men. The primary cause: absence of clitoral stimulation. Women who consistently receive clitoral stimulation are 38% more likely to orgasm every time.

Men's Anatomy & Pleasure Map
Male sexual anatomy is widely assumed to be simple. It isn't.
The Frenulum — The Most Overlooked Point

The frenulum is the V-shaped band of skin on the underside of the penis, where the foreskin meets the glans. It is the single most nerve-dense point on the male body and is considered the primary trigger of orgasm in most men — referred to as the "male clitoris" in anatomy research.

The Prostate (P-Spot)

A walnut-sized gland between the bladder and rectum. Research describes prostate-stimulated orgasms as potentially surpassing penile orgasms in intensity. Accessible externally via firm perineum pressure, or internally via the rectum.

The Perineum

The stretch of skin between the scrotum and anus. Densely packed with nerve endings and directly overlies the prostate. Firm external pressure here — particularly just before orgasm — significantly amplifies intensity.

The Glans (Head)

The most visible sensitive zone. Packed with nerve endings, particularly around the coronal ridge (the rim). Sensitivity varies — for some men the glans is intensely sensitive, for others indirect stimulation is preferable.

Scrotum & Testicles

The scrotum contains extensive nerve endings often ignored during sexual activity. Gentle cupping, light pulling, or warmth applied to the scrotum adds significant sensation during build-up to orgasm.

Non-Genital Erogenous Zones
  • Nipples — made of identical erectile tissue to the penis. Brain imaging shows nipple stimulation activates the same neural pathways as genital stimulation in many men
  • Inner thighs — build arousal and anticipation without direct stimulation
  • Neck and ears — highly responsive to breath, lips, and light touch
  • Lower back — connected to pelvic nerve clusters

The prostate has been called the male G-spot for good reason. It is the anatomical equivalent — deeply sensitive, widely ignored, and genuinely transformative when explored with patience and care.

The Hormone Map
Eight hormones regulate your sexual health, drive, and function.
Testosterone
Primary Drive Hormone · Both Sexes

The most important hormone for libido in both men and women. Controls desire, arousal, energy, and sexual confidence. Declines with poor sleep, chronic stress, obesity, and alcohol. Rises with strength training, quality sleep, zinc, and vitamin D.

Estrogen
Lubrication, Sensation & Mood · Women

Maintains vaginal tissue health, lubrication, and sensitivity. Levels fluctuate throughout the menstrual cycle — highest just before ovulation (peak desire window). Low estrogen causes vaginal dryness, reduced sensitivity, and discomfort during sex.

Cortisol
The Libido Killer · Both Sexes

The primary stress hormone. When chronically elevated, the body deprioritises sex hormone production. Chronic high cortisol suppresses testosterone in men, disrupts estrogen cycles in women, reduces lubrication, and lowers desire across the board.

Dopamine
Desire & Anticipation · Both Sexes

The neurotransmitter of motivation and reward. Drives sexual desire and the urge to pursue intimacy. Activities that dysregulate dopamine — excessive pornography, social media — can blunt natural sexual desire by raising the stimulation threshold.

Oxytocin
Bonding & Connection · Both Sexes

The bonding hormone. Released during touch, orgasm, eye contact, and physical closeness. In long-term relationships, oxytocin is a key driver of sustained desire and intimacy beyond the initial dopamine-driven phase.

Prolactin
Post-Orgasm Reset · Both Sexes

Rises sharply after orgasm, producing the refractory period. Chronically elevated prolactin (hyperprolactinaemia) can suppress testosterone, estrogen, and libido significantly. Certain medications and thyroid disorders can raise prolactin.

Progesterone
Cycle Balance · Women

Rises in the second half of the menstrual cycle. Generally has a dampening effect on libido. High progesterone from synthetic sources — some contraceptives — can significantly lower desire and lubrication.

Thyroid Hormones
The Silent Disruptor · Both Sexes

Hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid) is one of the most commonly missed causes of low libido. Symptoms include fatigue, weight gain, depression, and significantly reduced drive. A simple TSH blood test identifies it.

Cortisol is the single most controllable variable in your hormone profile. Addressing chronic stress is one of the most powerful things you can do for your sexual health.

Post-SSRI Sexual Dysfunction (PSSD)

A critically underreported condition where sexual dysfunction persists even after discontinuing SSRI or SNRI antidepressants. Symptoms include persistent genital numbness, anorgasmia, emotional blunting, and reduced desire. Can last months to years. If you experience this, speak with a specialist — awareness is growing and management options are emerging.

Zinc: genuine evidence for testosterone support in zinc-deficient individuals. Vitamin D: low levels consistently associated with lower testosterone.

Fluid Taste & Body Chemistry
Diet, hydration, and lifestyle measurably affect the chemical composition of bodily fluids.

Vaginal secretions and semen are both biochemically complex fluids whose composition is affected by diet, hydration, overall health, hormonal balance, and microbiome health. The vagina maintains a naturally acidic pH (3.8–4.5), regulated by Lactobacillus bacteria. Semen is naturally alkaline (pH 7.2–8.0).

Improves Taste
  • Hydration — the single most impactful factor
  • Fruits — pineapple, citrus, berries, melon
  • Cinnamon and parsley — reduce pungency
  • Yoghurt and fermented foods — support vaginal microbiome
  • Green vegetables — chlorophyll-rich foods reduce bitterness
Worsens Taste
  • Alcohol — directly alters fluid chemistry
  • Asparagus — asparagusic acid breaks into sulphur compounds
  • Red meat in excess — stronger, more pungent taste
  • Smoking — significantly alters all bodily secretions
  • Coffee and strong spices — temporary effect in excess

A sudden or significant change in the smell or taste of vaginal discharge is a medical signal, not a dietary problem. Speak with a doctor.

Natural Drive Boosters
Evidence-based lifestyle interventions with genuine impact on hormones and desire.
Sleep

The majority of daily testosterone production occurs during deep sleep. One week of sleeping 5 hours reduces testosterone by 10–15% in young men — equivalent to ageing 10–15 years. Quality sleep is a fundamental hormone regulation mechanism.

Strength Training

Compound movements (squats, deadlifts, presses) produce an acute testosterone spike post-workout and support sustained higher baseline testosterone. Equally effective for women. Aim for 3–4 sessions per week.

Pelvic Floor

In women: strong pelvic floor muscles increase orgasm intensity and enhance sensitivity. In men — almost never discussed — pelvic floor strength directly improves erectile quality and ejaculatory control. Kegel exercises: contract 5 sec, release, 10–15 reps, 3x daily.

Stress Management

Cortisol directly suppresses sex hormones. Sustained low-level pressure maintains elevated cortisol that consistently lowers desire, function, and satisfaction. Exercise, consistent sleep, and mindfulness are evidence-based approaches.

Sex Toys — Safe Use Guide
The sex toy industry is almost entirely unregulated. Most people don't know that "body-safe" has no legal definition.

Independent testing has found phthalates — endocrine-disrupting chemicals linked to reproductive harm — in products marketed as safe, at levels exceeding hazard thresholds. Knowing your materials is the only real protection.

✓

Safe Materials

Medical-grade / platinum-cured silicone · Borosilicate glass · Stainless steel · ABS hard plastic · Ceramic (glazed)

⚠

Use With Caution

TPE / TPR — porous, cannot be fully sterilised. Single-person use only. Always use a condom on porous toys.

✕

Avoid

Jelly rubber · PVC · "Silicone blends" · Latex (if sensitive) — porous, leach phthalates, cannot be sterilised

Lubricant Compatibility
  • Water-based lubricant — safe with all toy materials and all condoms. Best all-purpose choice
  • Silicone-based lubricant — excellent for glass and metal. Never use with silicone toys — degrades the material at a molecular level
  • Oil-based lubricant — safe for glass and metal only. Degrades latex condoms entirely. Disrupts vaginal pH — use externally only
Hygiene Rules
  • Wash toys before and after every use, without exception
  • Non-porous toys — boil 3 minutes, dishwasher (non-motorised), or 10% bleach solution
  • Porous toys (TPE/TPR) — cannot be fully sterilised. Use a condom on them. Replace regularly
  • Dry completely before storage — moisture creates bacterial and mould growth
  • Inspect before each use — cracks, discolouration, tacky surfaces = replace immediately
Anal Toy Safety — Non-Negotiable Rules
  • Flared base is mandatory — without it, objects can be lost internally, requiring medical intervention
  • Lubrication is mandatory — the anus does not self-lubricate under any circumstances
  • Start small, go slowly — the anal sphincter requires relaxation before penetration is comfortable
  • Never use a vaginal toy anally and then vaginally without thorough cleaning — introduces rectal bacteria

The best toy is one made from a safe material, kept clean, and used with appropriate lubrication. Quality matters — and so does maintenance.

When to See a Doctor
Self-education has limits. These are the signs that need professional assessment — not lifestyle changes.
Signs That Need Medical Attention
Sudden, significant, or unexplained drop in libido — particularly if persistent for more than 4–6 weeks
Painful sex (dyspareunia) — for any gender. Always warrants investigation, never normalise
Inability to orgasm that is new and unexplained (particularly if post-medication changes)
Erectile dysfunction — particularly if new onset, consistent, or occurring in younger men
Significant change in vaginal discharge — colour, odour, consistency, or volume
Unusual lumps, sores, blisters, or rashes on or around the genitals
Bleeding after sex, between periods, or post-menopause — always needs assessment
Persistent genital numbness, particularly following antidepressant use — may indicate PSSD
Signs of STIs: burning during urination, unusual discharge, pain, or confirmed exposure
Extreme fatigue, weight changes, and low libido together — may indicate thyroid dysfunction
Specialists to Know
  • Sexual health clinic / GUM clinic — STI testing, contraception, sexual dysfunction, confidential and non-judgmental
  • Gynaecologist — female reproductive and sexual health, hormonal concerns, pain conditions
  • Urologist — male sexual health, erectile dysfunction, prostate health
  • Endocrinologist — hormone imbalances, thyroid disorders, testosterone and estrogen issues
  • Pelvic floor physiotherapist — pain during sex, pelvic floor dysfunction, post-childbirth recovery
  • Sex therapist / psychosexual counsellor — psychological components, relationship dynamics, trauma-informed care

Seeking help for sexual health concerns is not embarrassing. It is one of the most informed, self-respecting things a person can do.
