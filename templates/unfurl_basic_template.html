Why This Guide Exists
Standard sexual health advice
often falls short.
Most resources assume one partner. This one doesn't.

If you have sex with multiple people, your risk profile, testing needs, and medication options are meaningfully different — and you deserve information built for your actual life.

The Standard Fails You
Annual STI tests. "Use a condom." No mention of PrEP, Doxy-PEP, suppressive therapy, or the fact that throat and rectal infections are almost always asymptomatic. This guide fills that gap.
01

Your testing schedule is different

Quarterly testing — not annual — is the CDC evidence-based standard for sexually active people with multiple partners.

02

Medications change everything

HIV prevention medications, post-exposure options, and suppressive therapies exist — most people don't know they can access them.

03

Body literacy is your first defence

Knowing what symptoms to watch for, and when to act, lets you catch infections before they become serious.

Chapter 02 — Barrier Methods
Barrier Methods — Correct Use Every Time

Barriers are most effective when used every time, for every type of sex, before any genital contact begins.

External Condoms

Using Correctly

  • 98% effective with perfect use — correct size matters
  • Apply before any genital contact, not just penetration
  • Use water-based lube with latex; silicone with polyurethane
  • Check expiry — never store in wallets or heat
  • One condom per act — do not reuse
Other Barriers

Full Coverage

  • Internal condoms: 95% effective; can insert hours in advance
  • Dental dams for oral sex on vulva or anus
  • Nitrile gloves for manual contact with cuts or skin breaks
  • Finger cots for individual finger protection
  • Barriers reduce risk — they do not eliminate it entirely

Mental Health & Stigma — Pillar 05
□

Health Anxiety

Obsessively checking symptoms is health anxiety — a real, treatable condition.

  • Stick to a fixed testing schedule
  • Avoid Googling symptoms obsessively
  • Testing resolves uncertainty
  • Consider therapy if anxiety affects your life
⊕

Positive Result ≠ Failure

STIs happen to careful, informed adults all the time. A positive result means treatment — nothing more.

  • Most STIs are fully curable
  • Your status says nothing about character
  • Shame delays treatment — refuse it
  • Find a non-judgmental provider
⊕

Sex-Positive Healthcare

Not all providers are comfortable with multiple partners. Finding the right one matters.

  • DrSafeHands.com — India-based
  • LGBTQ+ health centres: most affirming
  • Be direct about your number of partners
  • Switch if you feel judged

Chapter 06–08 — Communication, Tracker & Checklists
06
Pillar 06
Talking About Sexual Health
These conversations feel awkward the first few times — then they become completely normal.
1

Before any new sexual contact

Share your last test date and result. Ask the same. Standard practice — not interrogation.

2

Agree on what protection you'll use

Before things start, agree on barriers and for which acts. Don't assume.

3

Share test results as a habit

After every test, let sexual contacts know — even when all-clear. Make it routine.

4

If positive — disclose promptly

Contact everyone who may have been exposed. Most STIs are treatable. Early notification = faster care.

5

Use anonymous notification if needed

inSPOT.org offers free anonymous partner notification by text.

Opening the conversation

"I was last tested on [date] — all clear. When were you last tested?"

Before sex

"Before we go further, can we talk about what protection we'll use?"

Disclosing a positive result

"I just got my results — I need to let you know I tested positive for [X]. You should get tested."

Sharing your approach

"I'm on PrEP and test every 3 months. Here's my most recent result."

If someone refuses to disclose

Anyone who won't share their status is not giving you the information needed to consent. It's completely reasonable to decline.

Chapter 08 — Health Checklists
08
Practical Tools
Health Checklists
Consistent small actions compound into excellent long-term sexual health.

Quarterly

Every 3 months — your full health reset.

  • Full STI panel — HIV, gonorrhea, chlamydia, syphilis at all relevant sites
  • Herpes & Trichomonas (every 6 months minimum)
  • PrEP refill + kidney function labs if on daily oral PrEP
  • Restock barriers — condoms, lube, dental dams
  • Update contact tracker
  • Schedule any specialist visits
  • Mental health check-in

Monthly

Self-check between testing cycles.

  • Visual self-exam — any new sores, warts, rashes?
  • Any burning, unusual discharge, or pain?
  • Barrier supply check
  • Any symptoms to follow up on?
  • Cycle / hormonal changes if relevant
  • Any health anxiety you've been avoiding?

New Contact

Before sex with someone new.

  • Share your last test date and result
  • Ask for theirs
  • Agree on what barriers to use and for which acts
  • Discuss PrEP / prevention approach
  • Add to contact tracker after
  • 2-week buffer before unprotected sex (window period)

If You Test Positive

A positive result is information — not a verdict.

  • Start treatment immediately
  • Notify all recent contacts who may be exposed
  • Use inSPOT.org for anonymous notification
  • Pause unprotected sex until treatment complete
  • Identify the exposure window
  • No shame — you did the right thing by testing
